Carbon 14 method

The carbon-14 method is a process used in physics and archaeology to determine the age of organic objects. It works by measuring the amount of carbon-14 present in the object compared to the amount of stable carbon present.

Carbon-14 is a radioactive isotope of carbon that occurs naturally in the Earth's atmosphere. When living organisms absorb carbon dioxide, they ingest both stable and radioactive carbon, and over time, the amount of carbon-14 decreases while the amount of stable carbon remains constant.

Through the use of special equipment, scientists can measure the amount of carbon-14 present in an organic object and compare it to the known amount of carbon-14 present in the atmosphere at a certain point in time. From this measurement, they can calculate the approximate age of the object.

This method has been crucial in determining the date of many historical events and has allowed archaeologists and other researchers to have a better understanding of the past and how it has evolved over time.
