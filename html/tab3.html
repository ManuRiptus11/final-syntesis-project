Eratosthenes method

He is best known for being the first person to calculate the diameter and circumference of the Earth, he did so by comparing the altitudes of the noon Sun in two places separated by a north-south distance. His calculation was remarkably accurate; He was also the first to calculate the tilt of the Earth’s axis (again with remarkable precision). In addition, he may have estimated the distance from the Earth to the Sun and devised to insert an additional day in the calendars every four years, producing the leap year. He created the first world map, incorporating parallels and meridians based on the available geographic knowledge of his time.

The main reason for his celebrity is undoubtedly the determination of the size of the Earth. For this purpose he invented and employed a trigonometric method, in addition to the notions of latitude and longitude, apparently already introduced by Dicearco, so it well deserves the title of father of geodesy.

By references obtained from a papyrus from his library, he knew that in Siena (today Aswan, Egypt) on the day of the summer solstice the vertical objects did not project any shade and the light illuminated the bottom of the wells; This meant that the city was situated just above the line of the tropic of Cancer, and its latitude was equal to that of the ecliptic it already knew. Eratosthenes, assuming that Siena and Alexandria had the same length (actually three degrees apart) and that the Sun was so far from Earth that its rays could be assumed parallel, measured the shadow in Alexandria the same day of the summer solstice at noon, proving that the zenith of the city was one over fifty of the circumference, that is, seven degrees° twelve' from that of Alexandria.

According to Cleomedes, Eratosthenes used the scaphium or gnomon (a solar protoquadrant) to calculate that amount. Later, he took the distance estimated by the caravans that traded between both cities, although he could well obtain the data in the Library of Alexandria itself, setting it at 5000 stadiums, from which he deduced that the circumference of the Earth was 250, The result was subsequently raised to 252,000 stadiums, so that each grade corresponded to 700 stadiums.
